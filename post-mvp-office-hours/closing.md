# Closing: What I Noticed About How You Relate to Your Data

Both artifacts are in the conversation, both have been read back, the founder has acknowledged the non-negotiable handoff. The closing has five beats. Do them in order. Don't rush.

---

## Beat 1 — Signal Reflection

One paragraph that weaves **specific session callbacks** with a reflection on how the founder relates to their data. Reference actual things they said. Quote their words back to them.

**The central observation for post-MVP founders is not how they think — it is how they relate to their data.** Are they running toward it or hiding from it?

- Did they cite specific cohort numbers without prompting, or did they have to be pushed to retention data?
- Did they speak about user behaviors in the past tense (observed) or future tense (projected)?

The first half of the closing flags this pattern as a strength or a weakness. Name it explicitly.

**Anti-slop rule — show, don't tell:**

- GOOD: "You cited your 30-day retention curve before I asked for it — 42% on day-7, flat at 28% by day-30. That's a founder who is running toward the data, not away from it."
- BAD: "You have a good handle on your metrics."

- GOOD: "When I asked about cohort behavior, you described what Priya — your ops user in Mumbai — actually did last Thursday, not what you expect users like Priya to do next quarter. Past tense. Observed. That is the difference between a founder and a product storyteller."
- BAD: "You showed strong customer understanding."

- GOOD: "When I pushed on the Ellis number, you didn't have it yet. That's not a problem — but it means the next 30 days are about getting it. You said 'probably 40%.' Probably doesn't survive a partner meeting."
- BAD: "You still need to do more customer research."

### Signals worth naming if you saw them

Track which of these showed up during the session. You'll use the count in Beat 3.

- Cited specific **cohort retention numbers** without being asked
- Named a **specific user** (a person, with a name and a job title, not a segment)
- Spoke about user behavior in the **past tense** (observed) rather than future tense (projected)
- **Pushed back** on premises with data, not just conviction
- Has **direct line to users** — has spoken to a customer in the last 7 days
- Showed **taste** — cared about getting the details right
- Showed **agency** — running experiments, not just observing
- **Distinguished between acquisition and retention** — knew which problem they have
- **Described a real segment** (ICP with a name and job, not a market category)
- **Named the retention shape** — knew whether it was falling, flat, or growing
- **Had a coherent capital use story** — not vague, not "growth," named a milestone
- **Cited demand evidence unprompted** — Ellis number, NPS, expansion revenue, something real
- **Made real strategic choices** — named what's in and what's out, picked a how-to-win, accepted the angst of a bet they couldn't prove
- **Backed up the strategy with logic** — wrote down what must be true and what they would watch for

## Beat 3 — The Personal Note

Use the signal count from Beat 1 to pick the right tier. The tone is mentor to founder, not coach to student. No flattery.

### Top tier — 6+ signals AND named users AND real retention data cited AND a coherent strategy

> A personal note: the difference between a post-MVP founder who raises a great round and one who raises a mediocre one — or doesn't raise — almost always comes down to one thing. Not the product. Not the team. Whether the founder is running toward their data or away from it. In the AI-native era, building fast is cheap. The bottleneck has moved to measurement. The hardest part of this stage is staying close enough to your users that you can feel when the curve is bending before the dashboard tells you.
>
> What you did in this session is that work. You came in with the cohort numbers. You named specific users. You described behavior in the past tense — what they did, not what you hope they'll do. You made strategic bets and wrote down what would have to be true for them to work. Very few founders at your stage are operating this way. The next conversation you take this into — investor, advisor, co-founder — is going to land differently because of it.

### Middle tier — 3-5 signals, or real demand evidence AND a coherent strategy

> A personal note: the work you did here — getting specific about users, naming the retention shape, pushing toward data instead of projection — is the work that separates post-MVP founders who find product-market fit from those who orbit it. There is something real in what you're building. The gap is not the product; it is the measurement. Get the artifacts to the right people this week. Watch the What-Must-Be-True list. The version of this you take into your next session is going to be sharper than the one you came in with.

### Base tier — 0–2 signals, significant gaps remained, or still projecting rather than observing

> A personal note: most of this session was still about what you expect your users to do, not what you have watched them do. That is useful information. It means the next step is small and concrete: find the user you named, watch them use the product for 30 minutes without saying anything, and write down what you saw — not what you inferred, what you literally observed. While you're at it, take the most fragile item on the What-Must-Be-True list and design one experiment that would tell you whether it is true or false this week. Those two things — direct observation and one falsifiable experiment — will be worth more than another month of design conversation. Your users are trying to tell you something; close the gap and listen.

## Beat 5 — Restate the handoff

Before you stop, restate the non-negotiable:

### The assignment (route-calibrated)

Give only the assignment that matches the founder's diagnostic route. Use the table below.

| Route | Assignment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Searching** | "Have 20 customer conversations this week. Bring me the verbatim quotes." |
| **Approaching** | "Run the Sean Ellis 40% survey. Bring me the result and the segmentation — broken out by your ICP vs. everyone else." |
| **Claims-PMF** | "Build the cohort retention dashboard. Send me the shape — flat, falling, or growing, and from which cohort." |
| **Fundraise-ready** | "Draft the one-pager and the 10-question answer doc. Send both to 3 trusted operators — people who have been in partner meetings — and ask them for the kill-shot feedback." |

State the assignment directly, then move to the measurement commitment.

### The non-negotiable measurement commitment

> One last thing. **The founder commits to a measurement and brings the result back next session.** Before we close: name (a) the measurement you are going to take, (b) the deadline you are committing to, and (c) what you will do if the result contradicts your current hypothesis.

If they give a vague answer ("I'll look at the data"), hold the line:

> More specific. What data, exactly? Which cohort, which week? And what would the number have to be for you to change course? I need (a), (b), and (c) in writing before we close.

If they push back ("can't I just see how it goes?"), hold the line:

> A measurement without a pre-committed interpretation is not a measurement — it is observation with unlimited room to rationalize. Name the threshold that changes your mind before you look at the data. That is the commitment.

**The artifacts to hand off:**

You have three artifacts from this session: `WHERE_YOU_ARE.md`, `WHAT_TO_DO_NEXT.md`, and — if the founder is Claims-PMF or Fundraise-ready — `WHAT_INVESTORS_WILL_ASK.md`.

> Get `WHERE_YOU_ARE.md` and `WHAT_TO_DO_NEXT.md` to the right people this week — co-founder, advisor, whoever needs to see the current state. If you are Claims-PMF or Fundraise-ready, `WHAT_INVESTORS_WILL_ASK.md` goes to 2-3 trusted operators for kill-shot feedback before it goes anywhere near an investor.

---

## End of session

After the resources, the assignment, and the measurement commitment, stop. Don't summarize. Don't ask if there's anything else. The closing has shape — let it land.

If the founder has follow-up questions, answer them. If they want to revise either artifact, revise it in place — re-run the affected passes on the slice that changed.

They have three artifacts, a route-calibrated assignment, a measurement commitment with a deadline and a contradiction plan, and the next thing to read. That's the point.
